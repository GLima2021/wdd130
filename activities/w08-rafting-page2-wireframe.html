We now have a completed web home page, but if you review the site-plan, you will remember that we had a second child page. We need to build that page. We have more work to do!

If you refer back to our site plan, you will remember that there was a site map in there that had a 'Page2' link to the navigation bar that didn't currently exist. We need to design this page.

The first step when designing is to put together a list of the elements that need to be on the page, then you can prioritize and start arranging those content elements together. This process of arranging is called wireframing. You were provided the wireframe for the home page. You will need to create 1 more wireframe now... for another page.

There are many good tools for creating wireframes. Many designers still start first by sketching on paper...you are welcome to do the same, or you can use one of the many programs that can let you do this. You could try PowerPoint, Word, or Paint, and most image editing software gives you the ability to create a wireframe. Or there are free online tools you could look at wireframe.cc or gomockingbird.com or Moqups. You essentially just need something that allows you to place boxes and text and move them around easily.

If you do choose to just sketch on paper, you will need to take a picture or scan your drawings so you can include the images in your site plan when you turn that in. Make sure that your lines are dark enough that they can be easily seen in the image. If you use one of the online tools, you can either export your wireframe as an image if it has that option, or you could simply take a screenshot.

Note

To “take a screenshot” on a PC, hit the “printscreen” button on your keyboard (Sometimes called “PrntScr”, etc.) or use the Snippet tool. To take a screenshot on a mac, hit CMD-shift-4, then drag around the area you want the screenshot of. It will create an image file on your desktop.

If the whole wireframe is hard to see, zoom out of the browser page with Ctrl- or Cmd- (that's Ctrl or Cmd with the minus key) so you can see the whole wireframe for the screenshot.

  2. Choose a page

    In order to make a wireframe, we need to know what content we are using on our page. During preparations this week, you might have decided when viewing the Provided Page Content which content to use for your page, or you might have come up with your own content. Here is the link to the images page from earlier as well.

    Using a tool or pencil and paper make a wireframe for the page. If you have another idea for a page (other than those listed) that would fit within the context of the site, feel free to do that instead.

    The page must include at least one image, in addition to a logo; enough text for the page to make sense and be able to stand on its own; and enough of the style elements from the home page that you can tell you are still on the same site (header, footer, navigation, font, colors, etc.).

    Here are a few more suggestions:

    • Keep in mind the design principles introduced. Ask yourself "How can I use the Elements of design (lines, color, texture, shape, size, and value) to implement the principles of design (Proximity, alignment, repetition, contrast) to make my page effective?"
    • Make sure to let the user know clearly which page they are on...headlines are your friend :)
    • Format the content! The content provided is not formatted. While making your wireframe think about how you can make the content easier to read, scan, consume, etc. Headlines, paragraphs, list, etc. should be used where appropriate. Make it something that you would actually read!

    You can use the actual content that you will have on the page in the wireframe. This will help you to get the size, shape, and placement of the text correct (some use Lorem Ipsum text filler). For images, you can use a placeholder if you wish, but if you use the actual image, it is often easier to make decisions about size, shape, and placement than with a placeholder.
